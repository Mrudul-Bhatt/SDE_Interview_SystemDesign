# Video Streaming (like YouTube or Netflix)

---

## What Are We Building?

You open YouTube on your phone and tap a video. Within 2 seconds, the video starts playing.
You scrub forward to the 45-minute mark — it jumps there almost instantly. You move from
your couch (WiFi) to your car (4G), and the video keeps playing — it just looks a bit less
crisp for a few seconds while it adjusts.

```
YouTube scale:
  500 hours of video uploaded EVERY MINUTE
  2 billion users
  1 billion hours of video watched EVERY DAY
  About 15% of all internet traffic worldwide
```

Behind the scenes, we have to:
- Accept giant video files from creators (some uploads are 50 GB)
- Convert each video into multiple quality levels (4K, 1080p, 720p, 480p, 360p)
- Store all those versions across data centers worldwide
- Stream them to viewers' devices smoothly, even when their network gets bad
- Track view counts, resume positions, search, and recommendations

---

## Requirements (What the System Must Do)

### Functional (features)
```
1. Users upload videos (mp4, mov, avi, etc.)
2. System converts each video into multiple resolutions (360p → 4K)
3. Adaptive playback: quality adjusts to viewer's current network speed
4. Search videos by title, tag, category
5. Like, comment, subscribe, view count
6. "Resume from where you left off"
7. Subtitles and closed captions
```

### Non-Functional (scale and reliability)
```
Scale:    2 billion users, 800M videos total, 500 hours/min uploaded
Speed:    Video starts playing in under 2 seconds after click
          Seeking to any position takes under 1 second
Upload:   Even a 2 GB upload completes in under 60 minutes (99% of the time)
Storage:  Videos stored with "11 nines" durability (basically never lost)
CDN:      95%+ of playback served from edge servers near the viewer (not origin)
Uptime:   99.99% for playback
```

## How Much Hardware?

Before building anything, engineers estimate **how big the system needs to be**. This is called
"capacity planning" or "back-of-the-envelope estimation." Most systems worry about storage or
requests-per-second. Video streaming has a different monster: **bandwidth** — the sheer firehose of
bytes needed to push video to billions of screens at once. So our questions are:

1. **How much storage** for all the videos?
2. **How much bandwidth** to stream to viewers? (this is the real monster)
3. **How much compute** to transcode uploads?

Let's work through them, slowly.

---

### Question 1: How much storage?

Every minute, a flood of new video arrives — and each video is stored several times over (one copy
per quality level, plus the original). Let's measure it.

```
Step 1 — How many hours of video arrive per day?
  500 hours/min × 60 min × 24 hours = 720,000 hours uploaded per day

Step 2 — Each uploaded hour becomes SEVERAL files, not one.
  We transcode it into ~5 quality levels (360p…4K) AND keep the raw original.
  Roughly ~10 GB of storage per uploaded hour, all versions combined.

Step 3 — Storage added per day:
  720,000 hours × 10 GB ≈ 7 PB (petabytes) per day
  → over a year this grows into EXABYTES (1 EB = 1,000 PB)
```

**Worth noticing:** this is a lot, but storage is *cheap per byte* and these files are written once
and rarely changed. Big as it is, storage is **not** the hardest problem. The next one is.

---

### Question 2: How much bandwidth? (the real monster)

Storing a video once is easy. **Streaming it to millions of people at the same time is the brutal
part** — because every viewer is continuously pulling several megabits per second, and there are an
enormous number of viewers at once.

```
Step 1 — How many people are watching at peak?
  Of 2 billion users, suppose ~50 million are streaming at the same moment.

Step 2 — How much bandwidth does one stream need?
  ~5 Mbps for smooth 1080p video.

Step 3 — Total bandwidth to serve everyone:
  50,000,000 viewers × 5 Mbps = 250,000,000 Mbps = 250 Tbps (terabits/sec)
```

**The key insight: video streaming is *bandwidth-bound*.** 250 terabits per second is a
*staggering* amount of network traffic — no single datacenter on earth can push that out. This one
number is the entire reason a **CDN (Content Delivery Network)** exists: instead of serving all 250
Tbps from one origin, we copy popular videos to thousands of edge servers spread around the world,
each near its viewers, and let them share the load.

```
Why the "95% from CDN" requirement matters:
  If 95% is served from edge servers, only ~5% (≈12 Tbps) ever reaches the origin.
  237 Tbps is absorbed by edges close to viewers; origin handles the small remainder.
```

> Takeaway: size video streaming by *bandwidth to viewers*, not storage. The CDN exists purely to
> spread that 250 Tbps across the globe so no single point has to carry it.

---

### Question 3: How much compute to transcode?

Every uploaded video must be converted into all those quality levels (transcoding), and that's
CPU/GPU-heavy work. But here's the saving grace:

```
500 hours of video arrive every minute, and each must be transcoded into ~5
renditions → a large transcode farm (tens of thousands of CPU/GPU cores).

BUT: transcoding happens ONCE per video, at upload time.
That same transcoded video is then watched millions of times with zero extra
transcoding — playback just downloads the pre-made files.
```

**What this tells us:** transcoding is expensive but it's a **"cook once, serve billions" cost.**
You pay the CPU bill a single time when the video is uploaded; after that, every view is just a
cheap file download from the CDN. This asymmetry — modest one-time cooking vs. astronomical serving —
is the core economics of video streaming.

---

### The numbers to remember

```
~7 PB/day storage (→ exabytes)  → huge but cheap; written once, not the hard part
~250 Tbps bandwidth at peak     → the real monster; the ENTIRE reason CDNs exist
~95% served from CDN edge       → spreads that 250 Tbps globally so origin survives
transcode = "cook once"         → expensive once per video, then served free forever
```

The big idea connecting them: video streaming is defined by **bandwidth, not storage or compute**.
Delivering 250 Tbps to viewers is the dominant challenge, which is why the whole architecture is
built around a CDN — cook each video once, then serve its pre-made files from edge servers as close
to each viewer as possible.

## The Three Big Problems

Before we dive into the architecture, here are the three problems we need to solve:

```
1. UPLOAD: How do we accept a 50 GB file from someone whose WiFi might cut out?

2. TRANSCODING: A creator uploads 4K 60fps. A viewer on a 3G phone can't play that.
                We need to make many smaller versions in different qualities.

3. DELIVERY: 1 billion hours watched per day. We can't serve all of that from one
             data center. We need servers near every viewer in the world.
```

Let's tackle each one.

---

## Problem #1: How Do We Upload a Huge File?

Imagine uploading a 5 GB video from your phone over hotel WiFi. Halfway through, the WiFi
flickers. With a naïve upload, you'd start over from 0%.

### The Trick: Chunked, Resumable Uploads

Split the file into small chunks (5 MB each) and upload them one by one.

```
A 2 GB video becomes 400 chunks of 5 MB each.

Upload protocol:
  Step 1: POST /upload/init → server returns an upload_id + list of pre-signed URLs
  Step 2: For each chunk: PUT /upload/{id}/chunk/{n}  (with the bytes)
  Step 3: POST /upload/{id}/complete → server stitches chunks together
```

**Why this is great:**
- If chunk 47 fails, just retry chunk 47 — don't redo chunks 1–46
- Multiple chunks can upload in parallel (3–5 at a time) for speed
- Each chunk has a checksum, so we can detect corruption
- Pause and resume tomorrow if you want

The chunks land in S3 (Amazon's blob storage) as a single combined file once stitched.
S3 stores it with "11 nines" durability — meaning the chance of losing the file is
99.999999999%. You'd lose your house first.

---

## Problem #2: Transcoding — Making Many Versions

A creator uploads in whatever format their camera produced — maybe 4K H.265 at 60 fps.
But what about the viewer on a 3-year-old phone over a weak cell signal? They need
something much smaller and simpler to play.

So for every uploaded video, we produce **5–6 different versions** at different
quality levels.

### The Renditions (Versions) We Produce

```
Resolution    Bitrate       Used by
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
360p          400 kbps      Old phones, very slow networks
480p          800 kbps      Mobile, average networks
720p          2.5 Mbps      Default for most users
1080p         5 Mbps        Good WiFi, modern devices
4K            16 Mbps       Premium, very fast networks
```

### Why Multiple Renditions?

If we only stored one version (say 1080p), users on slow networks couldn't play it at all.
If we only stored 360p, premium viewers would get a blurry experience.

**Multiple renditions** let the viewer's player pick the right one for their current
network speed — and switch as conditions change.

### How Transcoding Works

```
Step 1: A transcoder worker (with a GPU) picks up an "upload complete" event from Kafka.
Step 2: Downloads the original video from S3.
Step 3: Runs FFmpeg to produce all renditions in parallel:
          - Convert codec (e.g., H.265 → H.264)
          - Resize (e.g., 4K → 720p)
          - Re-encode at the target bitrate
Step 4: Splits each rendition into 6-second segments (.ts files for HLS)
Step 5: Uploads all segments to S3
Step 6: Writes a "master playlist" that lists all available renditions
Step 7: Marks the video as READY in the metadata database
```

### How Long Does It Take?

```
For a 1-hour video:
  ~30 minutes per rendition on a 4-core CPU
  Running all 5 renditions in parallel on a GPU farm → ~10 minutes total

YouTube has thousands of transcoder machines running 24/7 to keep up
with 500 hours/minute of uploads.
```

### Cutting Into Segments — The Magic Trick

Each rendition isn't stored as one giant file. It's chopped into **6-second segments**:

```
seg000.ts   → seconds 0–6
seg001.ts   → seconds 6–12
seg002.ts   → seconds 12–18
...
seg600.ts   → seconds 3600–3606
```

**Why segments?** Two huge benefits:
- The player can switch quality between segments (we'll see this next)
- Seeking is just "fetch segment 450" — no need to download from the start

## Problem #3: Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (ABR)

Now the magic. Your network speed changes constantly — WiFi to 4G, train tunnels,
dropped signal. The video should keep playing without you noticing.

### The Manifest File (M3U8)

When you press play, the first thing your player downloads is a tiny text file called the
**manifest** (or "master playlist"):

```
#EXTM3U
#EXT-X-VERSION:3

#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=5000000,RESOLUTION=1920x1080
1080p/index.m3u8

#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=2500000,RESOLUTION=1280x720
720p/index.m3u8

#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=800000,RESOLUTION=854x480
480p/index.m3u8

#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=400000,RESOLUTION=640x360
360p/index.m3u8
```

This says "here are the qualities available, each at a known bandwidth requirement."

### How the Player Decides

```
Step 1: Start at a reasonable default (e.g., 720p).
Step 2: Download the first segment. Measure how long it took.
Step 3: Calculate throughput = (bytes downloaded) / (seconds taken)
Step 4: For the next segment:
          - If throughput is way higher than current bitrate → step up (try 1080p)
          - If throughput is dropping toward current bitrate → step down (try 480p)
Step 5: Always keep 15–30 seconds buffered ahead.
Step 6: If buffer falls below 5 seconds → emergency drop to lowest quality.
```

The player is constantly re-measuring and adjusting — once per 6-second segment.
You notice the quality shift if you're paying attention, but the video never stops.

### Why 6 Seconds?

- Shorter segments (2s) → faster quality adjustment, but 3× more HTTP requests
- Longer segments (10s) → fewer requests, but slow to react to network changes
- 6 seconds is the sweet spot that Netflix, YouTube, and most providers landed on

## The Architecture

```
─── Upload Path ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Creator's    Upload         Raw Video     Kafka          Transcoder
  device  ──▶  Service  ──▶   Store (S3) ──▶  events  ──▶  Workers (GPU)
                                                              │
                                                              ▼
                                                    HLS Segments in S3
                                                              │
                                                              ▼
                                                       Metadata DB updated
                                                       (video status = READY)

─── Playback Path ───────────────────────────────────────────────

  Viewer's    API Gateway  ──▶ Metadata DB (get video info)
  device  ──▶                  
                ▼
            CDN Edge (CloudFront, Akamai, etc.)
                │
         95% hit → return segment from edge (super fast)
         5% miss → fetch from origin (S3), cache at edge, return
                ▼
            Player plays segment, measures speed, picks next quality
```

## How Storage Works

```
Layer 1: Raw originals (S3 Standard for 30 days, then Glacier)
  - The original uploaded file, kept forever
  - If we ever need to re-transcode (new codec, fixed bug), we have it
  - Petabytes total

Layer 2: Transcoded segments (S3 with CDN in front)
  - All the .ts segments for all renditions
  - Replicated across regions (us-east, eu-west, ap-southeast)
  - These are what viewers actually download

Layer 3: Metadata (Cassandra)
  - video_id, uploader, title, description, tags
  - status (UPLOADING / TRANSCODING / READY / DELETED)
  - duration, view count
  - When videos were uploaded

Layer 4: User watch history (Cassandra)
  - partition key: user_id
  - clustering: watched_at DESC
  - lets us answer "resume from 23:45" and feed recommendations
```

## The CDN: Why Videos Feel Fast

A CDN (Content Delivery Network) is a network of servers spread across the globe.
When you request a video segment, the request goes to the **nearest** CDN edge — maybe
a server in your own city — instead of all the way to the origin in Virginia.

### How the Hit Rate Stays High

```
Popular videos (top 1% — viral content, breaking news, Netflix releases):
  - Pre-cached at every CDN edge worldwide
  - Cache TTL: 24 hours (segments are immutable, so we could cache forever)
  - Hit rate: 95%+

Long-tail content (the 90% of videos with few views):
  - NOT pre-cached
  - On first request at each edge: fetch from origin, cache, serve
  - Less popular at some edges → cache misses → fetched from regional origin
```

### Why Segments Cache Beautifully

Once a segment is created, it never changes. The URL `cdn.youtube.com/{video_id}/720p/seg042.ts`
will always return the exact same bytes. This means:
- We can cache it for years
- We can cache the same file at thousands of edges with no risk of inconsistency
- A "stale" version doesn't exist — it's just the segment

### Pre-Seeding for New Releases

Netflix puts physical appliances called **Open Connect** at major ISPs around the world.
The day before "Stranger Things" season X drops, Netflix copies the show to every
appliance overnight. When the season releases, the bytes are already sitting on a
server one network hop away from the viewer. Latency is essentially zero.

## View Counts: How Do We Count 1 Billion Hours/Day?

We can't increment a database row every time someone watches a second of video.
That's billions of DB writes per second. The database would melt.

### The Buffered Counting Pipeline

```
Step 1: Player sends a "heartbeat" every 30 seconds with: { video_id, position }
Step 2: An analytics service receives heartbeats and dumps them into Kafka (fire and forget)
Step 3: A stream processor (Flink) reads Kafka, aggregates per video per minute
Step 4: Every 60 seconds, batch flush: "video X got 10,432 new views this minute"
Step 5: Atomic counter update in Cassandra: UPDATE videos SET views = views + 10432
```

This pipeline handles billions of events per day with no contention. The view count is
slightly delayed (typically by 60 seconds), but nobody cares — "10.4M views" is good enough.

### What Counts as a View?

YouTube has specific rules:
- 1 view per user per 24-hour session (so reloading doesn't count multiple times)
- Bots are filtered: rate limit by IP, user-agent fingerprinting, minimum playback duration
- "Official" view count is reconciled nightly via a batch job against the raw Kafka log

### Trending Algorithm

```
trending_score = views_in_last_hour × recency_weight / (age_hours + 1)^gravity

→ A new video with rapid viewership beats an old video with the same hourly rate.
→ Same idea as Hacker News ranking.
```

## Search

When you type "funny cats 2024," it's not Cassandra answering. Cassandra is great for
key lookups but bad at full-text search.

```
When a video becomes READY:
  → Worker publishes metadata to Elasticsearch
  → Indexed fields: title (analyzed), description, tags, channel, category

Search ranking signals:
  - BM25 relevance (text match score)
  - View count (log-normalized)
  - Click-through rate from search results
  - Watch time percentage (people finish the good videos)
  - Recency boost for fresh content
  - Channel authority score
```

## Resume Playback

```
While playing:
  Every 10 seconds, client sends: PUT /watch-progress/{video_id} { position: 342 }
  Written to user_watch_history in Cassandra (async, non-blocking)

On video open:
  Client fetches: GET /watch-progress/{video_id}
  Returns: { position: 342, total: 1800 }
  Player seeks to position 342 (which means "fetch segment 57") before starting ABR
```

The seek is fast because each segment is independent. We don't need to download
seconds 0–342 to get to second 342 — just fetch the segment containing it.

## How It All Fits Together

```
─── Upload ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Creator hits Upload on a 2 GB video:
       │
       ├─ Client splits into 400 chunks of 5 MB
       ├─ POST /upload/init → gets upload_id + presigned URLs
       ├─ Uploads chunks in parallel (3 at a time)
       ├─ Retries failed chunks individually
       └─ POST /upload/{id}/complete

   Server: stitch chunks → publish "upload-complete" to Kafka

   Transcoder pool:
       ├─ Pick up Kafka event
       ├─ Download raw from S3
       ├─ FFmpeg: produce 5 renditions in parallel (~10 min on GPU)
       ├─ Cut each into 6-second segments
       ├─ Upload all segments to S3
       ├─ Write master M3U8 manifest
       └─ Mark video READY in metadata DB

─── Playback ───────────────────────────────────────────────────

Viewer clicks the video:
       │
       ├─ Player fetches metadata (title, etc.) from API
       ├─ Player fetches master M3U8 manifest
       ├─ Player picks a starting rendition (e.g., 720p)
       ├─ Player fetches the first segment via CDN
       │     - CDN edge serves it (cache hit 95% of time)
       │     - Or fetches from origin S3 on miss
       ├─ Measures download speed
       ├─ Picks rendition for next segment
       ├─ Maintains 15–30s buffer ahead of playhead
       └─ Every 30s: heartbeat to analytics service (Kafka)

Viewer pauses or closes:
       └─ Client writes current position to watch history (resume support)
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid (Interview Pitfalls)

```
❌ One transcoding queue, FIFO
   → A 3-hour 4K upload blocks all the 30-second clips behind it for hours
   ✅ Priority queues: short videos in their own queue with more workers

❌ Mutable segment URLs
   → Re-transcoding overwrites; CDN edges serve the OLD bytes for hours
   ✅ Segments are immutable; new versions get new filenames (version hash in URL)

❌ Counting views by updating Cassandra on every play
   → Hot row contention; database collapses
   ✅ Heartbeats → Kafka → stream aggregator → batch counter update

❌ Single CDN region
   → Viewer in Tokyo pulls from Virginia → 300ms+ first-byte latency
   ✅ Multi-region CDN with geo-DNS routing to nearest edge

❌ No bot filtering on view counts
   → Inflated view counts attract fraud (monetization at stake)
   ✅ 48-hour holding window; verify duration, IP variety, user-agent, playback patterns

❌ Viral video cold start
   → New video goes viral overnight; origin gets hammered before CDN warms up
   ✅ Pre-warm CDN on upload for trending channels; rate-limit origin per video

❌ ABR algorithm too conservative
   → Viewer has 100 Mbps but player stays at 720p forever
   ✅ Periodically probe higher renditions; commit when sustained throughput proves stable

❌ Losing the original after transcoding
   → Codec change in 5 years means we'd want to re-transcode but only have the lossy versions
   ✅ Keep the original in S3 forever (move to Glacier for cheap storage after 30 days)

❌ Synchronous transcoding during upload
   → Upload "completes" only after transcoding finishes (could be 30 minutes)
   ✅ Upload completes instantly; transcoding is async; video becomes playable when READY
```

## Real Systems at a Glance

```
YouTube:  Custom transcoding fleet on Borg/GKE; Vitess (sharded MySQL) for metadata;
          BigTable for watch history; global anycast CDN

Netflix:  Open Connect appliances physically deployed at ISPs; multi-region AWS;
          custom ABR (BOLA algorithm based on buffer occupancy); Chaos Monkey for resilience

TikTok:   Very short segments (1-3s) for fast start; aggressive prefetch of the NEXT video
          while you watch the current; HEVC encoding (50% smaller than H.264)

Twitch:   Live ingest via RTMP; edge transcoding nodes at each PoP for low-latency live;
          HLS with 2-second segments for ~6-second end-to-end latency
```
